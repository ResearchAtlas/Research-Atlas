Sample Workflow
Stage 1: Set up the review environment and role
First, we need to upload the full paper file. AI can read PDFs or Word documents directly, and thanks to its long context window, we do not need to split the paper into chunks. Uploading the entire paper helps preserve coherence and continuity of context. After uploading, we need to clearly define AI’s identity. We should not give a simplistic instruction like “read the paper.” Instead, we should set a specific role framework.
We enter an instruction asking AI to act as a senior reviewer for the target journal. We should specify the discipline, the tier of the target journal, and the review priorities. For example: “We are submitting to a top-tier computer vision journal. Please read this paper as a senior reviewer in this field.” This setup makes subsequent feedback more targeted and aligned with the academic standards of the specific field.

Sample prompt:
I have uploaded the full paper. Please act as a senior reviewer for a top-tier journal in the field of [insert specific discipline here] (e.g., [insert target journal name here]). Please conduct a deep reading of this paper strictly according to the academic standards and peer-review norms of this journal tier.
Your review should focus on the following priorities:
1.	Value of the topic: Evaluate whether the research question has important theoretical significance or practical application value.
2.	Logical rigor of the argument: Examine whether the entire process from hypothesis formulation to conclusion is rigorous and free of logical flaws.
3.	Rigor and completeness: Check whether the paper’s structure, citations, and data presentation follow internationally accepted academic norms.
Please do not output a detailed review report yet. For now, reply only with “Received”, and summarize in 3–5 sentences the core research question and main contributions as you understand them, so I can confirm you accurately captured the full paper.

Stage 2: Macro structure and logical chain validation
After confirming AI has read the document, we move to macro-level review. The goal here is to check whether the paper’s “skeleton” is complete and whether the logical chain is closed. We ask AI to extract the core contributions and summarize the main arguments. This step verifies whether our intended message is being communicated correctly. If AI’s summary deviates significantly from what we intended, it suggests problems in how the introduction or abstract is written.

Sample prompt:
Based on your understanding of the full text, extract and summarize the paper’s core contributions and main arguments. Do not speak in generalities. Complete the following tasks:
1.	Core contribution summary: List the 3–5 most important innovations or contributions, using concise language.
2.	Argument restatement: In one paragraph, clearly restate the core scientific question the paper tries to solve and the main conclusion it reaches.
3.	Deviation self-check: If you find any sections where the writing makes the core intent ambiguous, or where the emphasized points do not follow standard logic, explicitly point out the specific sections or paragraphs.
My purpose is to use your summary to verify whether the paper accurately communicates my research intent. Please be objective.
Next, we instruct AI to review logical coherence across sections. We focus on whether the “problem raised in the introduction” is addressed in the “methods,” and whether the “conclusion” matches the “abstract.” We can ask questions like: “Are the research hypotheses sufficiently supported by experimental data? Does the discussion explain results with rigorous logic?” This helps quickly locate structural breaks or logical jumps.

Sample prompt:
Conduct a deep macro-level logical review of the paper to assess whether the overall structure is complete and whether the reasoning forms a closed loop. Evaluate the coherence of the paper along these dimensions, with specific analysis:
1.	Alignment between problem and method: Do the specific research questions raised in the Introduction have clear and feasible solutions in the Method section? Are there cases where the methods fail to cover the stated problems?
2.	Support for hypotheses by data: Are the initial hypotheses sufficiently supported by the experimental data? Identify any weak points where the evidence is not strong enough to support the hypotheses.
3.	Rigor of results and discussion: Does the Discussion logically and rigorously interpret the Results? Are there any overclaims or unclear explanations?
4.	Consistency between abstract and conclusion: Does the Conclusion fulfill what the Abstract promised? Are there cases where the conclusion drifts away from, or fails to address, the abstract’s key points?
Please respond point-by-point. If you find logical gaps or jumps, quote the relevant text to illustrate.

Stage 3: Deep content critique and novelty assessment
After structural checks, we shift to deep critique of the content. This simulates the most demanding part of real peer review. We ask AI to identify potentially fatal weaknesses, specifically challenging the experimental design, data analysis methods, or theoretical derivations.
Sample prompt:
Please continue acting as a senior reviewer and conduct the most severe critical review of this paper’s methodology and interpretation of results. Do not be polite. My goal is to uncover every potential reason for rejection. Focus on the following:
1.	Methodological limitations: List 3–5 major limitations in the experimental design, data collection, or analysis methods. Are there uncontrolled confounders? Is the sample size sufficient to support statistical significance?
2.	Overclaim check: Review the Discussion and Conclusion and identify claims that go beyond what the data actually supports. Which supposed causal relationships are only correlations? Which inferences lack a solid evidence chain?
3.	Likely attack points: If a skeptical expert read this paper, what flaw would they most likely attack? Explain specifically and provide possible defenses or revision suggestions.
We can also ask AI to compare the paper to classic literature or the latest SOTA methods in the field to assess novelty. Asking “Is the marginal contribution significant enough compared to similar work?” helps us anticipate sharp reviewer critiques.
Sample prompt:
Place this paper in the current disciplinary context and objectively evaluate its novelty and academic contribution. Perform the following comparative analyses:
1.	Comparison to SOTA: Compare the proposed method/theory to currently recognized state-of-the-art work or classic references in the field. Does it offer significant advantages in performance, efficiency, interpretability, or scope?
2.	Marginal contribution: Relative to existing similar studies, where exactly is the real incremental contribution? Is it “mainly applying existing methods,” or does it “introduce a genuinely new perspective/architecture”? Judge whether the contribution level supports publication in a top-tier journal.
3.	Novelty rating: On a 10-point scale (10 = disruptive innovation), give a novelty score and explain why points were deducted. If novelty is insufficient, provide concrete suggestions to increase theoretical depth or technical value.

Stage 4: Language quality and formatting consistency optimization
The final stage focuses on language and academic conventions. Even strong research can be rejected due to language issues. We use AI’s multilingual ability to polish writing. For non-native writers, we instruct AI to identify phrasing that is not natural academic English and provide revisions.

Sample prompt:
Act as a professional copy editor at a top academic publisher. I will send you one paragraph from the paper. Do the following:
1.	Academic rewrite: Identify and fix expressions that are not idiomatic academic English (including awkward collocations, overly conversational phrases). Replace them with more precise and formal academic wording.
2.	Increase objectivity and concision: Remove overly subjective, emotional, or redundant modifiers (e.g., “very,” “extremely,” “surprisingly”) to ensure an objective tone. Break down or restructure long, complex sentences to improve clarity.
3.	Side-by-side feedback: Provide the revised text, plus a brief “edit notes” section explaining key terminology or structural changes so I can learn and verify.
Get ready. Here is the paragraph to polish:
[paste paragraph here]

We do not only want grammar fixes, but improved clarity and concision. We can send paragraph-by-paragraph instructions like: “Rewrite this paragraph to be more academic and objective, removing redundant modifiers.” We can also ask AI to verify figure/table numbering and check whether reference formatting is consistent. This ensures the paper meets publication standards and reduces negative impressions caused by formatting.

Sample prompt:
Perform a final formatting and citation consistency check (sanity check) on the paper. Focus on the following details and list every potential issue you find:
1.	Figure/table reference verification: Check whether “Fig. X” or “Table Y” mentioned in the text matches the surrounding context. Are there cases where the text says “as shown in Fig. 3” but the paragraph is actually discussing Fig. 2?
2.	Reference list formatting: Check whether the reference list (References) is consistent in formatting (e.g., author name order, placement of year, journal abbreviation rules).
3.	Dangling citation check: Find in-text citations (e.g., [12] or (Smith, 2023)) and confirm each has a matching entry in the reference list. Also check whether the reference list includes any entries that are never cited in the text.
Point out the exact locations and content of errors. Do not rewrite the whole paper, only output an “issue list.”

 
Core Frameworks for AI-Assisted Research
To transition from passive AI usage to "algorithmic competence," researchers should adopt structured frameworks that ensure rigor and reproducibility.
• Prompt Engineering Frameworks:
    ◦ COSTAR: Use this for conceptual tasks (writing, theorizing). It stands for Context, Objective, Style, Tone, Audience, and Response.
    ◦ TIDD-EC: Use this for technical or data-processing tasks to reduce variance. Components: Task, Instructions, Do, Don't, Examples, Content.
    ◦ Hybrid Prompting: For statistical reasoning, use "hybrid prompting" (combining explicit instructions, reasoning scaffolds, and format constraints) rather than zero-shot prompts to ensure assumptions are checked.
• The "Human-in-the-Loop" Mandate:
    ◦ AI should function as a "probabilistic partner," not a replacement for human judgment.
    ◦ Automation Bias: Researchers must actively resist the psychological tendency to over-rely on computer-generated advice or assume it is objective.
    ◦ Decision Authority: In fields like social work or medicine, AI is a consultant; humans must remain the final decision-makers to adhere to ethical standards.
Verification and Integrity Protocols
AI models are "stochastic engines" prone to hallucination and inconsistency. Verification is the primary defense against research misconduct.
• The "High-Five" Verification Protocol:
    1. Replicate: Run the prompt on a different model to see if the output holds.
    2. Verify Citations: Never assume a citation is real; click every link and search every DOI.
    3. Check Reasoning: Verify logical steps and arithmetic manually.
    4. Check for Bias: Explicitly ask the model to critique its own response for "blind spots."
    5. Traceability: Ensure information can be traced to a primary source.
• The "Hallucination Hunt":
    ◦ Researchers should treat every AI output as a hypothesis rather than a fact.
    ◦ Use Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) tools (like Elicit or Scite) that ground answers in uploaded documents rather than the model's training data.
• Audit Trails:
    ◦ TRAIL (Transparent Reporting of AI Logistics): Document every instance of AI use, including the specific tool, model version (e.g., GPT-4o), date, exact prompt used, and human modifications.
    ◦ QFATT-AI: A forensic audit framework to track research decisions and methodological fidelity when using AI.
Ethical Guardrails and Safety
Ethical use centers on data privacy, algorithmic bias, and authorship accountability.
• Data Privacy "Traffic Light" System:
    ◦ RED (Stop): Personally Identifiable Information (PII), Protected Health Information (PHI), confidential case notes, or raw interview transcripts. Never paste into public LLMs.
    ◦ YELLOW (Caution): De-identified demographics or aggregated tables. Proceed only if the vendor has a no-training agreement.
    ◦ GREEN (Go): Public literature, code syntax, methodology descriptions, and empty templates.
• Algorithmic Bias and "Red Teaming":
    ◦ AI models are trained on internet data and can reproduce structural biases (racism, sexism, classism).
    ◦ Red Teaming: Researchers should intentionally try to "break" the model or trick it into revealing bias (e.g., asking for risk assessments of marginalized groups) to identify and mitigate prejudice before using the data.
• Authorship and Disclosure:
    ◦ Not an Author: AI cannot be listed as an author because it cannot take legal responsibility for the work.
    ◦ Disclosure: Use must be disclosed in the Methods or Acknowledgments section, specifying the tool and how it was used.
    ◦ Liability: Humans are fully liable for any plagiarism or fabrication generated by the AI.
Domain-Specific Considerations
• Social Work & Human Services:
    ◦ Avoid "distributional collapse" where AI simulates "average" human behavior, erasing the nuance of vulnerable populations.
    ◦ Be wary of "deficit framing" or coded language in AI-generated risk assessments.
    ◦ Adhere to NASW standards regarding technology and client privacy.
• Quantitative Research:
    ◦ Synthetic Data: AI can generate synthetic datasets for pilot testing code, allowing researchers to practice without risking real client data.
    ◦ Simulation Risks: "Silico" subjects (AI simulated participants) should be used to test instruments, not to generate evidentiary claims about human behavior.
• Qualitative Research:
    ◦ AI can assist with coding and pattern recognition but requires a "verification playbook" to prevent hallucinated themes.
    ◦ Hybrid workflows (human + AI) are preferred over fully automated coding to maintain construct validity.
